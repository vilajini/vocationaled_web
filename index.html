Literature Survey

Exploring existing research and approaches

Artificial Intelligence in Education: Artificial Intelligence (AI) is revolutionizing many sectors, and its application in education is becoming increasingly prominent. In vocational education, however, this integration is still at an early stage.

Since vocational training focuses heavily on hands-on skills and job-readiness, it is essential that any AI system used in this context supports not just theoretical instruction but also practical competency.

Research by Liu et al. (2021)[1] illustrates how AI-driven educational platforms can personalize instruction, adapt content difficulty, and track learner performance in real time.

Prominent AI-powered learning management systems, such as Squirrel AI in China and Carnegie Learning in the United States, have already demonstrated success in improving learner engagement, knowledge retention, and academic performance by adapting to each learner’s pace and ability.

Challenges in Vocational Adoption: Despite these advancements, the adoption of AI in vocational training—especially in developing regions like Sri Lanka—is still limited. Many training institutions lack the digital infrastructure, internet bandwidth, or technical expertise required to implement such systems.

This creates a growing need for lightweight, context-aware, and scalable AI tools that are compatible with national vocational qualification frameworks, such as Sri Lanka’s NVQ.

AI and Oral Assessments: One of the most complex and labor-intensive components of vocational education is the oral viva. Viva sessions, typically used to assess practical knowledge and problem-solving ability, are usually conducted by instructors in-person.

These assessments are time-consuming, difficult to scale, and often subjective. Natural Language Processing (NLP), a subset of AI, offers a compelling solution to these challenges.

Speech-to-text technology combined with NLP enables AI systems to simulate real-time oral exams, evaluate spoken responses, and provide consistent feedback. Mohan et al. (2022)[3] found that NLP-based models can evaluate the semantic content and coherence of verbal answers with high accuracy. While such systems have shown success in standardized English proficiency exams, there is a notable lack of domain-specific AI-Viva systems, especially for vocational subjects like domestic electrician training. Moreover, limited support for regional languages such as Tamil and Sinhala remains a key challenge.

Gamification and Motivation: Gamification is another educational innovation with tremendous potential in vocational education. It involves incorporating game elements—such as rewards, levels, challenges, and leaderboards—into learning environments to increase motivation and engagement.

Research by Su et al. (2019)[24] confirmed that students who practiced vocational tasks through gamified simulators demonstrated better practical performance than those trained using traditional methods.

Need for Personalization: Personalized learning paths are essential for vocational learners, who often enter programs with varying levels of prior knowledge, experience, and confidence.

A standardized approach to content delivery fails to accommodate this diversity, resulting in disengagement and high dropout rates. Personalized systems use machine learning algorithms to analyze assessment results and classify students into levels such as Beginner, Intermediate, or Advanced.

Language and Accessibility: Language accessibility is another critical concern in Sri Lanka’s education sector. With Tamil, Sinhala, and English as the country’s primary languages of instruction, any effective learning system must provide multilingual support.

According to Alrashidi et al. (2022)[7], multilingual educational platforms improve learner comprehension, inclusivity, and retention. Technologies like Google Speech-to-Text support Sinhala and Tamil input, but vocational training-specific vocabulary and dialects require additional customization.

In conclusion, while AI, NLP, gamification, and personalized learning systems have shown great promise in academic environments, their application in vocational education—particularly in developing regions—remains insufficiently explored. This research aims to fill the gap by creating a multilingual, AI-driven training and assessment system that combines dynamic oral assessments, game-based learning modules, and adaptive skill-based learning paths to modernize vocational education in Sri Lanka.

Research Gaps

Key challenges in current vocational education systems

Limited AI Integration
Vocational education in countries like Sri Lanka lacks AI-powered tools, especially for automating oral assessments. Existing methods are manual, inconsistent, and not scalable.
Missing Gamified Learning
Static resources dominate. There’s a shortage of domain-specific, interactive 2D/3D tools to simulate real-life vocational tasks like wiring and circuit repair.
No Personalized Learning Paths
One-size-fits-all content delivery doesn’t adapt to different skill levels, leaving beginners overwhelmed and advanced learners unchallenged.
Language and Accessibility Gaps
Most systems ignore Sinhala/Tamil support and are not designed for low-literacy, mobile-first learners—excluding many rural students.
Need for a Unified Solution
A combined system offering AI viva simulation, adaptive content, and gamified learning—delivered in an inclusive, multilingual interface—is urgently needed.

Research Methodology

Our approach to solving the problem

The system initiates with the registration of learners and the assignment of a unique digital identity. Using voice interaction and speech-to-text, students participate in AI-driven oral assessments. Their responses are analyzed using NLP and dynamically scored in real time.

The gamified module enables learners to interact with vocational simulations (e.g., wiring circuits, safety protocols). Meanwhile, performance data is logged and analyzed by a classification engine to adapt each learner’s path—categorized as Beginner, Intermediate, or Advanced.

All components operate via a multilingual interface (Sinhala, Tamil, English) and are optimized for mobile and offline use in under-resourced areas, ensuring inclusivity and accessibility.
